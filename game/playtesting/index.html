Key Takeaways

  1. Balance your time! Playtesting vs. Development.
  2. Use Specific Questions for Playtesting.
  3. Qualitative Data is just as good as quantitative data.

Playtesting Plan

Playtesting was at the core of our production timeline. In fact, our weekly schedule was centered around preparing for, executing, and reviewing playtests.

From the start of our project, we made playtesting a priority because of a few reasons: Firstly, we aimed to design a platformer, which demands a considerable amount of design work to not just design and iterate on fun & unique mechanics and levels. Secondly, our Player’s Leaf Dash was unconventional, so we needed to iterate and refine it a lot to ensure it created interesting gameplay scenarios. Lastly, we only had a short 7-week production period to create the entire game, which left less time to naturally discover design trends. To expedite our design discovery, we needed to iterate faster to “find the fun” as soon as possible.

Inspiration

Our playtesting schedule & format drew heavily from the principles of “iterative game design”, outlined in Colleen Macklin and John Sharp’s book Games, Design and Play, as well as Valve’s playtesting philosophy outlined in Mark Brown’s article Valve's "Secret Weapon". We took these ideas to their extreme by playtesting every week and outlining specific goals for what we wanted to test for each playtest.

Schedule

Our weekly schedule was as follows: From Monday through Wednesday, we prepared a prototype by adding and tweaking features. On Thursday, we playtested with participants from our playtest interest form and took notes on player behaviors & discussions. After playtesting, we compiled our notes into quantitative counts of behavior patterns. On Fridays after our end-of-week work showcase, we had a “playtesting report” where we gave an overview of our compiled data and suggested possible changes or additions to tackle any design issues.

Pros and Cons

Our playtesting plan successfully helped to guide our game design, but came at the cost of taking away development time and pressuring the team to rush changes out for playtesting builds.

The biggest reason for this were our one-week turnarounds for playtesting, which proved to be a double-edged sword. On one hand, this ensured that we would have a playable build every week, which was good and helped give us a sense of progress over time. Weekly playtests also enabled us to easily course-correct by getting immediate feedback on changes or additions to the game. However, one-week turnarounds also pressured us to rush in additions to ensure we had something playtestable, which led to increased technical debt and stress on the team. The tight timeline also forced us to weigh what feedback we wanted to address from the last playtest versus what new content we wanted in the next build. We could not always tackle both of these.

Our method for gathering data was effective in some ways, but redundant in others. Our passive approach to notetaking helped us note genuine player behaviors and showed us where many design pain points were. Including a discussion period afterwards let us supplement our notes with plenty of qualitative feedback and suggestions. On the flip side, our tight timeline meant we had very little time to create relevant questions for our playtests. Instead, our questions remained broad.

We compiled our playtesting data by transcribing our qualitative notes into quantitative numbers. This proved to be ineffective because the process detached the data from the important context and player bias surrounding it, leaving the actual numbers devoid of meaning. The compilation process was also very time-consuming, since every noted player behavior had to be transcribed into numbers. Ultimately, all of our design decisions came from our existing qualitative notes rather than by analyzing our quantitative data points.
