About

We completed this project as part of Worcester Polytechnic Institute’s One-Term Studio. This means that all five core team members worked in a lab space 40 hours a week for 8 weeks, whereas Pre-Production was conducted in the prior academic term, working 8-10 hours a week.

For more information about our Pre-Production process check out the Pre-Production Page!

Development Timeline

The Development Timeline is a document we outlined during Pre-Production to plan out our Production schedule. We referenced and updated the timeline throughout Production to monitor progress and keep ourselves on track.

Our general timeline was as follows

  • Week 1: Project Set up and Documentation
  • Week 2: Prototype / Tech Demo
  • Week 3: First Playable
  • Week 4: Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
  • Week 5: Feature Complete
  • Week 6: Content Complete
  • Week 7: Documentation Complete
  • Week 8: Polish, Post Mortem, Project Complete

We intentionally added a 2-week buffer into our schedule. We intended for our game to be fully finished by the end of week 6. This was for two reasons:

  1. Save time for polish, post mortem, and documentation
  2. If anything about development doesn’t go as expected, we have 2-weeks built in as “roll-over” time (time to pivot and change project direction)

If you want more details on how each week breaks down, check out our Development Timeline!

Sprint Spreadsheet

This is a tool we created in Pre-Production, to use and update throughout Production. It’s purpose was to break down our timelines into actionable tasks for each team member.

Of note, we had several tabs, including:

  • Current Sprint
  • Backlog
  • Finished
  • Personal Tabs

Personal Tabs was something we implemented partway into Production. After every sprint set up meeting, each team member copied their specific tasks into their own spreadsheet tab to keep themselves organized. To ensure the Current Sprint was up to date, part of our Sprint Showcase was reviewing all tasks in the Current Sprint and updating their status.

Sprint Structure

We used a sprint system, splitting up our 8 weeks of Production into 8 sprints. Every day began with a short check-in (“Daily Standup”) to communicate progress with each other. Each week we held contractor check-ins on Mondays and Thursdays, conducted playtesting on Thursdays, and held a Sprint Showcase on Fridays.

Daily Standup

Daily Standups were short meetings every morning. As a team, we would each answer these questions:

  • What did you accomplish yesterday?
  • What will you work on today?
  • Do you need anything from another team member?

Once we all answered those questions, we would have a short team discussion, before breaking off for work the rest of the day.

Playtesting is a crucial part of the Production process. We established playtesting as part of the weekly sprint structure before Production began.

Sprint Showcase & Set Up

We held sprint showcases at the end of every sprint to:

  • Celebrate and review all of the team's progress for the week
  • Boost team morale by making jokes and having fun
  • Review the playtesting report
  • Set up the following sprint
  • If applicable, play the current game build
  • Update project advisors on our progress

This was critical to our development structure for the sake of communication and following our timelines.

Lessons Learned

  1. Establish a time buffer for game polish and documentation.
  2. Address conflict as soon as possible.
  3. Organizational structures should work for us, so update them as needed.

How We Learned This

Lesson 1

During Pre-Production, we established a two-week buffer in our development timeline. If anything during Production didn’t go as planned, we built in time to extend our development.

It’s better to have the extra time and not need it, than need it and not have it.

However, those final two weeks were originally intended for game polish and finishing our documentation. Since we extended our development into those two weeks, our allotted time for polish and writing was tighter.

We recommend future projects plan to completely finish their game (including polish and documentation) by a certain deadline. Give your team a time buffer where you have nothing planned. Best case scenario, you follow the development plan precisely and can evaluate how to use your free time when it arrives. Otherwise, use that time to finish anything that did not get completed.

Lesson 2

A good rule of thumb for any project. Especially important due to our condensed 8-week timeline. If conflict was allowed to fester, it would divide project members and the project would suffer. Our producer acted as the conflict point-person. If anyone had any issues with another team member, they could go to them to talk about it. It was the producer’s job to address the issue as soon as possible (often within 24 hours). We also had another team member as a secondary point of contact, in case the producer was involved in said conflict.

Lesson 3

Nothing about production should be completely set in stone. If a system isn’t working or making sense to the team, then we can change it!

For example, we established our Playtesting Pipeline very early, which included creating a build by 5pm the day before playtesting. However, partway through production, we realized this deadline created a lot of extra pressure for our team. If we missed a feature in a build which we needed to test, it would have to wait a week. Thus, we discussed, and agreed to push the build deadline to 10am the day of playtesting.
